# Biology - Cell Respiration

## Overview

Cell respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose to produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the primary energy currency of the cell. This process occurs in three main stages: glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation.

## Glycolysis

Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm and does not require oxygen. One molecule of glucose (6 carbons) is split into two molecules of pyruvate (3 carbons each). The net yield is 2 ATP and 2 NADH molecules. This process involves 10 enzymatic steps.

## Krebs Cycle

Also known as the citric acid cycle, the Krebs cycle takes place in the mitochondrial matrix. Each pyruvate is first converted to acetyl-CoA, releasing CO2. The cycle produces 2 ATP, 6 NADH, and 2 FADH2 per glucose molecule.

## Oxidative Phosphorylation

Oxidative phosphorylation occurs on the inner mitochondrial membrane. NADH and FADH2 donate electrons to the electron transport chain, which pumps H+ ions across the membrane. This creates a proton gradient that drives ATP synthase to produce approximately 32-34 ATP molecules.

## Summary

The total yield of cellular respiration per glucose molecule is approximately 36-38 ATP. Aerobic respiration is roughly 18 times more efficient than anaerobic fermentation, which only produces 2 ATP.
